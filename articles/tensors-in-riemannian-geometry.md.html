Tensors in Riemannian Geometry

Saturday 22 October 2022

This article is for people who are taking a course in Riemannian geometry but are a bit confused by all the various things you may or may not be able to do with tensors. I’ll include some explicit and easy computational examples to ground things. But I’m expecting the reader to know at least the Riemann curvature tensor and Riemann curvature endomorphism is.

Let’s first look at the vector space level. Let V be a real vector space of finite dimension, with V* its dual. Then a tensor of rank (k,l) is a map $$ \underset{l\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to \R $$ that is linear in each argument.

The set of all (k,l)-tensors on V is denoted Tlk(V). If v1, …, vn is a basis for V, then a (k,l)-tensor is defined precisely by its action on the basis elements. Therefore, a tensor is defined by the numbers

For two tensors F ∈ Tlk(V) and G ∈ Tnm(V), we define their tensor product $F\tensor G$ to be the map defined by $$ (F\tensor G)(\om^1,\ldots,\om^{l+n},X_1,\ldots,X_{k+m}) \\ := \\ F(\om^1,\ldots,\om^l,X_1\ldots,X_k)G(\om^{l+1},\ldots,\om^{l+n},X_{k+1},\ldots,X_{k+m}) $$ where $\om^1,\ldots\om^{l+n}\in V^*$ and X1, …, Xk + m ∈ V. Note that $F(\om^1,\ldots,\om^l,X_1\ldots,X_k)$ and $G(\om^{l+1},\ldots,\om^{l+n},X_{k+1},\ldots,X_{k+m})$ are just real numbers, so the product on the right hand side of the above definition is just multiplication of real numbers.

Ex: Show that $F\tensor G: \underset{l+n\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k+m\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to \R$ is linear in each argument, so that we do in fact have $F\tensor G\in T^{k+m}_{l+n}(V)$,

Ex: Come up with an example to show that the product of tensors is not necessarily commutative.

Lemma: There is a basis-independent (i.e. “natural”) linear isomorphism between Tl + 1k(V) and the space of multilinear maps $$ \underset{l\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to V. $$ Proof: We will construct this isomorphism in a basis independent manner. Call this isomorphism $$ \Phi : \cbracket{\underset{l\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to V} \to T^k_{l+1}. $$

Let $B:\underset{l\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to V$ be multilinear.

For $\om^1,\ldots,\om^{l+1}\in V^*$ and X1, …, Xk ∈ V define $$ (\Phi B)(\om^1,\ldots,\om^{l+1},X_1,\ldots,X_k) \\ := \om^{l+1}\rbracket{B(\om_1,\ldots,\om^l,X_1,\ldots,X_k)}. $$ Note that ΦB is linear in each argument because when restricting to each argument, we have a composition of linear functions.

Now, Φ is injective: ΦB ≡ 0 if and only if $\om^{l+1}\rbracket{B(\om_1,\ldots,\om^l,X_1,\ldots,X_k)}=0$ for each $\om^1,\ldots,\om^{l+1}\in V^*$ and for each X1, …, Xk ∈ V. This is the case if and only if $B(\om_1,\ldots,\om^l,X_1,\ldots,X_k)=0$ for all inputs, which is the case if and only if B ≡ 0. Thus ker Φ is trivial and so Φ is injective.

On the other hand, we saw before that dim Tl + 1k(V) = nk + k + 1, and similarly we have that $$ \dim\cbracket{\underset{l\textrm{ times}}{V^*\times\cdots\times V^*}\times\underset{k\textrm{ times}}{V\times\cdots\times V} \to V} = n^{k+l+1}. $$ Ex: The map Φ is linear.

Thus Φ is a linear map between vector spaces of the same dimension, with trivial kernel, and so Φ is a linear isomorphism. QED

The trace of a tensor

Now, let F ∈ Tkl(V) be a tensor. Let $\om^1,\ldots,\om^{l-1}\in V^*$ and X1, …, Xk − 1 ∈ V. Then $$ F(\om_1,\ldots,\om_{l-1},\cdot,X_1,\ldots,X_{k-1},\cdot)\in T^1_1(V) $$ is a map $V^*\times V\to\R$ and so is naturally isomorphic (by the above lemma) to some linear map F : V → V. This map F : V → V has a trace, which is defined in a basis-independed manner. In this way, we can define the trace of a tensor $\tr:T^k_l(V)\to T^{k-1}_{l-1}(V)$ as $$ \tr(F)(\om_1,\ldots,\om_{l-1},X_1,\ldots,X_{k-1}) \\ := \\ \tr\sbracket{\inv{\Phi}\rbracket{T(\om_1,\ldots,\om_{l-1},\cdot,X_1,\ldots,X_{k-1},\cdot)}}. $$

All this in terms of a basis

So far we’ve done all this in a basis-independent manner, but it’s certainly useful to introduce a basis to make this more concrete.

Let E1, …, En be a basis for V, and E1*, …, En* the corresponding basis for V* (i.e. Ei*(Ej) = δji).

E.g.: Consider a (1,2)-tensor F ∈ T21(V). So F is a map $V^*\times V^*\times V\to\R$, and F is specified precisely by its action on the bases. There are n basis elements for the first copy of V*, n basis elements for the second copy of V*, and n basis elements for V, and so F is specified by n3 numbers. For illustrative purposes, take n = 2. Then F is specified by 23 = 8 real numbers:

$$ F(E_1^*,E_1^*,E_1) =: F^{1,1}_1 \qquad F(E_1^*,E_1^*,E_2) =: F^{1,1}_2 \\ F(E_1^*,E_2^*,E_1) =: F^{1,2}_1 \qquad F(E_1^*,E_2^*,E_2) =: F^{1,2}_2 \\ F(E_2^*,E_1^*,E_1) =: F^{2,1}_1 \qquad F(E_2^*,E_1^*,E_2) =: F^{2,1}_2 \\ F(E_2^*,E_2^*,E_1) =: F^{2,2}_1 \qquad F(E_2^*,E_2^*,E_2) =: F^{2,2}_2. $$ That is, F is specified by its action on all possible combinations of the basis elements.

Now that we have a basis, we have an explicit isomorphism $V\iso V^*$, where $E_i\mapsto\tilde{E_i}$ for each i (and extend linearly also). With respect to these bases, we have the tensors $$ \tilde{E_1},\ldots,\tilde{E_n}:V\to\R\quad\textrm{where}\quad\tilde{E_i}(E_j):=\delta^i_j \\ \tilde{E_1^*},\ldots,\tilde{E_n^*}:V^*\to\R\quad\textrm{where}\quad\tilde{\phi^i}(\phi^j):=\delta^j_i $$ Notice that $\tilde{E_i}= E_i^*$, and since $V\iso V^**$ is a natural isomorphism, we can conflate $\tilde{E_i^*}:=E_i^{**}$ with Ei. In summary:

  • $\cbracket{E_1,\ldots,E_n}$ is a basis for V
  • $\cbracket{E_1^*,\ldots,E_n^*}$ is the corresponding basis for V*
  • $\cbracket{E_1^{**},\ldots,E_n^{**}}$ is a basis for V** and are tensors $\tilde{E_i^*}\in T^0_1(V)$; but Ei** are naturally isomorphic to Ei so we often denote Ei** as Ei.

The upshot: We can use Ei and Ei* to generate a basis for T^k_l(V)$.

E.g.: Consider F ∈ T21(V) given by F(E1*,E1*,E1) = F11, 1 = 2F(E1*,E1*,E2) = F21, 1 = 3F(E2*,E1*,E2) = F22, 1 = 5 with the rest zero. Then we can write $$ F = 2 E_1\tensor E_1\tensor E_1^* + 3 E_1\tensor E_1\tensor E_2^* + 5 E_2\tensor E_2\tensor E_2^*. $$ To see what is going on, compute F11, 1, F21, 1, and F22, 1 from the above expression for F and the definition of the tensor product.

Ex: Prove, by giving an explicit basis, that dim (Tlk(V)) = nk + l.

Now let’s take the trace of this tensor. Let om ∈ V*. Then $$ \tr(F)(\om) = \tr \Phi^{-1} F(\om^1,\cdot,\cdot), $$ where Φ is the isomorphism in the before lemma.

In order to take the trace we need to describe $F(\om,\cdot,\cdot)$ as an endomorphism V → V. Since Ei* is a basis for V*, we have $$ \om = \om_1 E_1^* + \om_2 E_2^* $$ for some $\om_1,\om_2\in\R$. Then $$ F(\om,\cdot,\cdot) = 2\om_1 E_1\tensor E_1^* + 3\om_1 E_1\tensor E_2^* + 5\om_2 E_1\tensor E_2^* $$ which is equivalent to the endomorphism given by the matrix $$ \begin{pmatrix} 2\om_1 & 3\om_1 \\ 5\om_2 & 0 \\ \end{pmatrix} $$ whose trace is $2\om_1$. Thus $\tr(F)(\om) = 2\om_1 = 2 E_1(\om)$ and so $$ \tr(F) = 2E_1 $$ which is indeed a (0,1) − tensor, as required!

Some geometry

Now let’s apply this linear algebra to geometry. Let M2 be a surface, p ∈ M a point on this surface, and consider the tangent space TpM and the cotangent space Tp*M. Then $$ \pderiv{}{x^1}|_p, \pderiv{}{x^2}|_p \quad\textrm{form a basis for }T_pM \\ \d{x^1}_p,\d{x^2}_p \quad\textrm{form a basis for }T_p^*M. $$ Suppose M has a Riemannian metric g. This means that g is a (2,0)-tensor field which is symmetric and non-degenerate at each point. That is, at each point p ∈ M we are given a (2,0)-tensor gp such that

  • $g_p(\pd_1,\pd_2) = g_p(\pd_1,\pd_2)$
  • gp(X,X) = 0 ⇔ X = 0.

Here, $\pd_i = \pderiv{}{x^i}|_p$ is shorthand.

So at p, the metric gp is specified by 22 = 4 numbers $$ g_p(\pd_1,\pd_1) = g_{1,1} \qquad g_p(\pd_1,\pd_2) = g_{1,2} \\ g_p(\pd_2,\pd_1) = g_{2,1} \qquad g_p(\pd_2,\pd_2) = g_{2,2}. $$ Except, of course, gp is only specified by three numbers because symmetry gives g2, 1 = g1, 2. So we can write the metric at a point as $$ g_p = g_{1,1}\d{x^1}_p\tensor\d{x^1}_p + g_{1,2}\d{x^1}_p\tensor\d{x^2}_p \\\quad + g_{2,1}\d{x^2}_p\tensor\d{x^1}_p + g_{2,2}\d{x^2}_p\tensor\d{x^2}_p \\ = g_{1,1}\d{x^1}_p\tensor\d{x^1}_p + 2g_{1,2}\d{x^1}_p\tensor\d{x^2}_p \\\quad + g_{2,2}\d{x^2}_p\tensor\d{x^2}_p $$ Letting p vary, we get functions $g_{i,j}:M\to\R$ (which are smooth). We write $\d{x^i}\tensor\d{x^j}$ as $\d{x^i}\d{x^j}$, and $\d{x^i}\tensor\d{x^i}$ as $\d{x^i}^2$ for notational convenience, giving $$ g = g_{1,1}\d{x_1}^2 + 2g_{1,2}\d{x^1}\d{x^2} + 2g_{2,2}\d{x^2}^2 = g_{ij}\d{x^i}\d{x^j} $$ using Einstein’s notation.

Note that we can’t (yet) take the trace of a Riemannian metric because we can’t (yet) take the trace of (2,0)-tensors. In order to do this we’d need to use musical isomorphisms.

So let’s explore another common tensor, the Riemannian curvature endomorphism.

Recall that the curvature endomorphism $$ R:\smooth{TM}\times\smooth{TM}\times\smooth{TM}\to\smooth{TM} $$ given by $$ R(X,Y)Z = \conn_X\conn_Y Z - \conn_Y\conn_X Z - \conn_{[X,Y]} Z. $$ Let’s focus at a point p ∈ M. Then we have Rp : TpM × TpM × TpM → TpM which by the before lemma we can turn into a (3,1)-tensor $$ T_p^*M\times T_pM\times T_pM\times T_pM\to \R $$ given by $$ R_p(\om, X, Y, Z) = \om(\conn_X\conn_Y Z - \conn_Y\conn_X Z - \conn_{[X,Y]} Z) $$ with the coefficients $$ \rbracket{R^i_{jkl}}_p = R_p(\d{x}^i_p,\pd_j|_p,\pd_k|_p,\pd_l|_p) $$ which a priori are 24 = 16 in number, but after considering symmetries of the curvature endomorphism are actually fewer.

Let’s try to take the trace of this tensor. We have $$ R = R^i_{jkl} \pd_i\tensor\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k}\tensor\d{x^l}. $$ This is a (3,1)-tensor so its trace will be a (2,0)-tensor. Let X, Y ∈ TpM with $$ X = X^1\pd_1 + X^2\pd_2 Y = Y^1\pd_1 + Y^2\pd_2. $$

Then $$ R(\cdot,X,Y,\cdot) = R^i_{jkl} X^jY^k\pd_i\tensor\d{x^l} $$ which has trace Rjk11XjYk + Rjk22XjYk which is, of course, the Ricci curvature.

Musical isomorphisms

Having been given a metric, which is an inner product at each tangent space, we no longer need a basis in order to produce an isomorphism $T_pM\iso T_p^*M$. Therefore using this metric we can reshuffle a, say (4,0)-tensor into a (3,1)-tensor and then take its trace. Of course we could reshuffle this tensor to a (2,2)-tensor and then take its trace, and we’d have to check that the trace is still the same.

Let’s start at the vector space level. Let V be an n-dimensional vector space with inner product $\inner{\cdot,\cdot}$. Then every element in the dual space $\a\in V^*$ has a unique representative a ∈ V such that $\a = \inner{a,\cdot}$. Thus the inner product induces a linear isomorphism of vector spaces V → V*, $a\to\inner{a,\cdot}$.

The inner product on V also induces an inner product on V*: $$ \inner{\a,\b}_{V^*} := \inner{a,b} $$ where a, b ∈ V such that $\a=\inner{a,\cdot}$ and $\b=\inner{b,\cdot}$.

In fact, these are exactly what we call the musical isomorphisms: $$ \flat:V\to V^* \qquad a^\flat := \inner{a,\cdot} \sharp:V^*\to V \qquad \a^\sharp := a. $$ We will soon elucidate this notation and convention.

Indeed, let $X = X^i\pd_i \in T_pM$ and $\om = \om_i \d{x^i} \in T_p^*M$. Then $$ T_pM \overset{\sharp}{\underset{\flat}{\rightleftarrows}} T_p^*M $$.

On the one hand, X♭ is the element of Tp*M defined by X♭ := g(X,⋅) so we have $$ X^\flat = g(X,\cdot) = g_{ij}\d{x^i}\d{x^j}(X,\cdot) = g_{ij}X^i\d{x^j}. $$ Thus if X♭ is given in coefficients by $X^\flat=X_j\d{x^j}$ then Xj = gijXi, so we have “lowered the indices”, and hence the flat ♭ : TpM → Tp*M.

On the other hand, $\om^\sharp$ is the element of TpM defined by $\om = g(\om^\sharp, \cdot)$. So we have $$ \om = g(\om^\sharp,\cdot) = g_{ij} (\om^\sharp)^i\d{x^j}. $$ That is, if $\om$ is given in coefficients by $\om = \om_i\d{x^i}$, and $\om^\sharp$ by $\om^\sharp = \om^i\pd_i$ then $$ \om^i = g^{ij}\om_j $$ (where of course gij are the coefficients of the inverse matrix $\inv{(g_{ij})}$), so we have “raised the indices”, hence the sharp ♯.

Now let F ∈ T;k(V). We can raise any of the l covector indices and lower any of the k vector indices.

E.g.: If $F = F^i_{jk}\pd_i\tensor\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k}\in T^1_2$, then we can

  • raise the second index: $$ F^{ij}_k = g^{il}F^i_{lk}\pd_i\tensor\pd_j\tensor\d{x^k}\in T^2_1 $$
  • raise the third index: $$ F^{i\quad k}_j = g^{kl} F^i_{jl}\pd_i\tensor\pd_k\tensor\d{x^j}\in T^2_1$$
  • lower the first index: $$ F_{ijk} = g_{il}F^l_{jk}\pd_i\tensor\pd_j\tensor\pd_k\in T^3. $$

E.g.: The metric is a (2,0)-tensor $$ g = g_{ij} \d{x^i}\tensor\d{x^j}. $$ We can raise either index (it doesn’t matter which by symmetry) to get $$ g^\sharp = g^{il}g_{lj} \pd_i\tensor\d{x^j} $$ which is a (1,1)-tensor, the trace of which is gilgli = δii = n, the dimension of the manifold(!). Thus $$ \tr(g) := \tr(g^\sharp) = n. $$ To be fair, this is a fairly trivial example.

E.g.: On the other hand, let’s do a much less trivial example. Recall the curvature endomorphism $$ R^i_{jkl}\pd_i\tensor\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k}\tensor\d{x^l}. $$ We can lower the first index to get the curvature tensor $$ R_{ijkl} = g_{im}R^m_{jkl} R = R_{ijkl}\d{x^i}\tensor\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k}\tensor\d{x^l} $$ which you may be more familiar with. The trace of this (4,0)-tensor is by definition the trace of the endomorphism, which is by definition the Ricci curvature: $$ \tr((4,0)\textrm{-tensor curvature tensor}) \\ = \tr((3,1)\textrm{-tensor curvature endomorphism}) \\ = \tr(R^i_{jkl}\pd_i\tensor\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k}\tensor\d{x^l}) \\ = R^i_{jki}\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k} \\ = Rc_{jk}\d{x^j}\tensor\d{x^k} $$ We can go further now at take the trace of the Ricci curvature $$ Rc = R_{ij}\d{x^i}\tensor\d{x^j} $$ but this time we need a musical isomorphism to raise an index: $$ S = \tr(Rc) = \tr(Rc^\sharp) = \tr(g^{il}\R_{lj}\pd_i\tensor\d{x^j}) = g^{il}R_{li}. $$

Note that each time we define a curvature using a trace which requires a musical isomorphism, we must first prove that it doesn’t matter which index we raise or lower. These curvatures (Ricci, scalar) are only well-defined because the various tensors involved (metric, Riemannian curvature tensor, Riemannian curvature endomorphism, Ricci curvature) have symmetries that indeed mean that it doesn’t matter which index we raise or lower.
