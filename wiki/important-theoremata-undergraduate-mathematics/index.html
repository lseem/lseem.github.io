Introduction

In this overview article, I list some theoremata that I think should summarise an undergraduate mathematics degree.

Often, I can get overwhelmed by the details of a course, and so I find it useful to just sit down and list the main results. Usually, they only take up half a page. Then, I focus on figuring out the proofs of these main results, and that’s pretty much all I need to know for the course: there’s no need to actively try to remember the definition of some obscure object, because chances are it’s been defined just to be able to prove a theorem (there are some important definitions, but I’ll probably end up remembering them just by the sheer number of times I use them while learning the subject).

Therefore, I intend to do this with a standard, undergraduate degree in pure mathematics. If you know and understand the objects and proofs involved in these theoremata, then you pretty much have an undergraduate degree in maths.

I will update this list every now and again.

  • Riemann mapping theorem
  • Seifert van-Kampen theorem
  • Stoke’s theorem
  • Mayer-Vietoris
  • Hilbert Nulstellensatz
  • Louiville’s theorem
  • Hahn-Banach theorem
