Introduction

The motivation for this algebraic structure comes from abstracting out important features of the Mayer-Vietoris exact sequence.

Definitions

A sequence of abelian groups and homomorphisms

\[ A_n \overset{d_n}{\longrightarrow} \cdots \longrightarrow A_{i+1} \overset{d_{i+1}}{\longrightarrow} A_i \overset{d_i}{\longrightarrow} A_{i-1} \longrightarrow \cdots \overset{d_1}{\longrightarrow} A_0 \]

is exact at \(A_i\) if \(\ker d_i = {\rm im}d_{i+1}\). This only makes sense for \(0<i<n\).

We say that the above sequence is exact if it is exact at \(A_i\) for all \(0<i<n\).

Elementary Properties

Here are some elementary propositions that you should prove yourself and internalise:

  • A sequence \(A\overset{f}{\to}B\to0\) is exact if and only if \(f\) is surjective.
  • A sequence \(0\to B\overset{g}{\to}C\) is exact if and only if \(g\) is injective.
  • A sequence \(0\to A\overset{f}{\to} B\to 0\) is exact if and only if \(f\) is an isomorphism.
  • A sequence \(0\to A\overset{f}{\to} B\overset{g}{\to} C\to0\) is exact if and only if
    • \(f\) is injective; (this is exactness at A)
    • \(g\) is surjective; (exactness at C)
    • \(f\) induces an isomorphism \(A\cong\ker g\). (exactness at B)
      • \(g\) induces an isomorphism \({\rm coker,} f\cong C\). (exactness at B phrased in a different way)
  • A homomorphism \(f:A\to B\) induces an exact sequence \(0\to\ker f\to A\to {\rm im}f\to0.\)

Connections to Chain Complexes

If we have an exact sequence

\[ \cdots\longrightarrow A_{n+1} \overset{d_{n+1}}{\longrightarrow} A_n \overset{d_n}{\longrightarrow} A_{n-1} \longrightarrow \cdots \]

defined for all \(n\in\mathbb{Z}\). Since \({\rm im\,}d_{n+1} \subset \ker d_n\) it follows that \(d_nd_{n+1} = 0\), and so \(A_\bullet\) is in fact a chain complex. Furthermore, since \({\rm im\,}d_{n+1}\supset\ker d_n\) it follows that \(B_n(A_\bullet) = Z_n(A_\bullet)\). So the homology groups of \(A_\bullet\) vanish in every degree \(H_n(A_\bullet)=0\)!

We conclude that an exact sequence is a chain complex where all its homology groups vanish. Indeed, a chain complex \(A_\bullet\) is exact at \(A_n\) if and only if \(H_n(A_\bullet) =0\).

Above we described a chain complex as an exact sequence. But we can also have exact sequences of chain complexes themselves. Indeed, a short exact sequence of chain complexes is a pair of chain maps

\[ A_\bullet \overset{i}{\longrightarrow} B_\bullet \overset{j}{\longrightarrow} C_\bullet \]

such that

\[ 0\longrightarrow A_n\overset{i}{\longrightarrow} B_n\overset{j}{\longrightarrow} C_n \longrightarrow0\]

is an exact sequence for all \(n\). A short exact sequence of chain complexes gives rise to a long exact sequence of homology groups.

Prop: Suppose we have a short exact sequence of chain complexers

\[ A_\bullet \overset{i}{\longrightarrow} B_\bullet \overset{j}{\longrightarrow} C_\bullet. \]

Then there is a long exact sequence of homology groups

\[ \cdots\longrightarrow H_{n+1}(C_\bullet)\overset{\partial}{\longrightarrow}H_n(A_\bullet)\overset{i}{\longrightarrow}H_n(B_\bullet)\overset{j}{\longrightarrow}H_n(C_\bullet)\overset{\partial}{\longrightarrow}H_{n-1}(A_\bullet)\longrightarrow\cdots .\]

Proof: We split the proof into four parts. Firstly, we prove exactness at \(H_n(B_\bullet)\). Then we define \(\partial\), and then we prove exactness at \(H_n(A_\bullet)\) and \(H_n(C_\bullet)\).
