Introduction

The motivation for the concept of a chain homotopy is to abstract the prism operator to a more algebraic setting.

Note that this diagram is not a “commutative diagram”, but since \(f\) and \(g\) are chain maps, there are two commutative “sub”-diagrams.

Definition

If \(C_\bullet\) and \(D_\bullet\) are chain complexes, and \(f,g:C_\bullet\to D_\bullet\) are chain maps, then a *chain homotopy* from \(f\) to \(g\) is a colleciton of homomorphisms \(P:C_n\to D_{n+1}\) such that for all \(n\),

\[ dP + Pd = g - f \]

as a map \(C_n\to D_n\).

Lemmata

In the following lemmata, the general set-up is that \(C_\bullet, D_\bullet\) are chain complexes, and \(f,g:C_\bullet\to D_\bullet\) are chain maps.

Lemma: If \(f\) and \(g\) are chain homotopic, then as maps \(H_n(C_\bullet)\to H_n(D_\bullet)\), \(f=g\). The proof of this lemma is very similar to the equaivalent lemma about the prism operator.

Proof: Let \(\sigma + {\rm im}\{d:C_{n+1}\to C_n\}\in H_n(C_\bullet)\). Then \(\sigma\in\ker\{d:C_n\to C_{n-1}\}\), and so \(d\sigma=0\). Since \(f\) and \(g\) are chain homotopic, we have that

\[ g(\sigma) - f(\sigma) = dP(\sigma) + Pd(\sigma) = dP(\sigma) \in{\rm im}\{d:C_{n+1}\to C_n\}. \]

Therefore, passing to homotopy under the quotient, \(g(\sigma)\) and \(f(\sigma)\) are in the same equivalence class, and so as maps \(H_n(C_\bullet)\to H_n(D_\bullet)\), \(f\) and \(g\) are equal.

QED.
